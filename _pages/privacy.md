You’re being watched online.

Every website quietly records a few signals:

- You opened this page.
- You clicked that link.
- You stayed here for three minutes.
- You arrived from somewhere else.

Those signals get bundled together and sent to advertising networks and analytics platforms. Over time they form a profile: what you read, what you like, what you might buy, what might keep you scrolling.

In software we’d call something like this telemetry, and use it measure servers. Surface information, trace events, debug failures. In that framing it seems neutralish, but at the end of the day telemetry on humans is fraught and unreliable at best (humans aren't deterministic, consistent, or contained the way a server is) and downright creepy at worst.

## The default web

Most websites participate in this system by default.

- Tracking scripts
- Ad networks.
- Behavioral analytics.
- “Engagement” dashboards.

Sometimes there’s a cookie banner pretending to ask permission.

Usually the real choice is simple:

Accept the tracking, or leave the site.

Even then, by the time you see the banner, some of the tracking has already happened.

## My politics, applied to software

I’m a leftist.

Part of what that means, to me, is believing that people shouldn’t be quietly extracted for value without their consent.

That principle usually shows up in conversations about labor, housing, healthcare, and economic systems. But it applies to technology too.

The modern web runs on surveillance because surveillance is profitable. The more precisely companies can observe people, the more precisely they can manipulate behavior&mdash;whether that behavior is buying something, clicking something, or believing something.

Visitors become data.

Data becomes targeting.

Targeting becomes revenue.

I’m not interested in participating in that system on my own website.

## This website does not track you

No cookies.<br>
No advertising trackers.<br>
No behavioral analytics.<br>
No fingerprinting scripts.

## Measurement (maybe)

At some point I may use a privacy-respecting analytics tool that provides simple aggregate numbers&mdash;things like how many people visited the site or which pages are popular&mdash;without collecting personal information.

But even that is optional.

Sometimes you don’t need to measure everything.

## The short version

You can read this website without being monitored.

That should be normal.
